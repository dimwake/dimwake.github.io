The Paper Story

We all use paper.

We write on it, fold it, scribble our thoughts when no one’s watching. Sometimes, we use it to clean messes. Other times, we pour our poetry onto it. But never do we ask the paper if it’s ready.

It never resists.

Some days, we crush it in frustration. Not because it did anything wrong— but because we were trying. Trying to say something right. Trying to make it fit.

And when the storm passes, we open it again. It’s still there. Quiet. But now, it holds wrinkles. Creases where patience met pressure. Smudges where truth tried to settle. Tears, maybe but small ones.

Still, it stayed. Still tried. But maybe it always absorbed too much. Maybe it made things harder. Maybe it let go too easily when held too tightly.

Some call that softness. Others call it weakness.

Not every hand learns to be soft. But maybe not every surface is meant to hold weight.

The more we wrote, the more we needed, the more it struggled to carry what it once could.

And then, one day, the paper tears. Not loudly. Not out of anger. Just… quietly.

No final protest. No grand failure. Just the slow unraveling of something that was never built to last.

And we’re left staring, not at a victim, not at a villain, but at the quiet truth:

Maybe we expected too much. Or maybe it was never enough to begin with.
